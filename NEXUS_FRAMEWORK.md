The loop can be applied at four scales:
- **World** — the whole setting
- **Area** — a region, city, biome, or zone
- **Faction** — a political, cultural, or ideological group
- **NPC** — an individual character

Each scale feeds the next. World → Area → Faction → NPC.

## Step 1 — Define the World

Establish the fundamental nature of the setting before adding any details. Everything else must be consistent with these answers.

### Core Questions

1. What is the **genre** of this world? (fantasy, sci-fi, horror, post-apocalyptic, contemporary, hybrid, etc.)
2. What is the **central theme** or premise? (survival, empire-building, mystery, decay, rebirth, etc.)
3. What is the **time period** or technological/magical era?
4. How old is this world, civilisation, or setting? What major **eras** preceded the current one?
5. What is the **current state** of the world? (stable, declining, at war, recovering, stagnant, etc.)
6. What are the **primary sources of conflict**? (resource scarcity, ideology, racial/species tension, cosmic threat, etc.)
7. What is **unique** about this world compared to genre defaults? What subverts expectations?
8. Who are the **dominant species or civilisations** and how did they rise?
9. What events in the **recent history** (last 100 years, relative to setting) shaped the current situation most?
10. What does **ordinary life** look like for the average person in this world?

### Scale Application

When applying to an **Area**, substitute "world" with the area and answer relative to that zone.  
When applying to a **Faction**, answer from that faction's founding myth and ideology.  
When applying to an **NPC**, answer as their personal origin story and worldview.

## Step 2 — Detail Geographical Data

Geography is destiny. The physical world determines what resources exist, how populations move, and what conflicts are natural vs. manufactured.

### Core Questions

1. What is the **overall shape and size** of the setting? (continent, island chain, underground labyrinth, orbital station, etc.)
2. What are the major **biomes or climate zones**? (desert, arctic, tropical, etc.)
3. What are the primary **water sources**? (rivers, lakes, seas, underground aquifers, ice)
4. What **natural barriers** exist? (mountain ranges, impassable forests, toxic zones, void rifts)
5. What are the **natural chokepoints** — places that control movement and trade?
6. How do **seasons** work, if at all? Are there unusual cycles (two suns, no seasons, perpetual storm season)?
7. What **natural resources** are abundant? What is scarce?
8. Where do **most people live** and why there specifically?
9. How does **terrain difficulty** affect travel and communication between regions?
10. Are there **unstable or contested** geographical zones (borders, resource deposits, sacred sites)?

### Consistency Check

- Resources should drive economic and political systems (Step 5).
- Barriers should explain cultural isolation and dialect drift (Step 7).
- Chokepoints should explain existing infrastructure (roads, fortifications, trade hubs).

## Step 3 — Specify Flora and Fauna

Ecosystems make worlds feel alive. They also provide resources, hazards, symbols, and cultural touchstones.

### Core Questions

1. What are the **dominant plant types**? How do they affect the visual landscape?
2. What **crops or harvestable plants** form the basis of food supply?
3. Are there **dangerous or toxic plants**? What makes them dangerous?
4. What **large fauna** exists? Are they prey, predator, or both?
5. What fauna is **economically important** (draft animals, food, materials, mounts)?
6. What **apex predators** exist and how do they affect settlement patterns?
7. Are there **domesticated species**? What was domesticated and why those species specifically?
8. What **insects, parasites, or microbial threats** exist? How do they affect health and food supply?
9. Are there **supernatural, alien, or magically altered** creatures? How do they interact with the ecosystem?
10. What **symbolic or cultural significance** do specific species hold?

### Consistency Check

- Fauna should influence available careers (Step 6): hunters, herders, pest controllers.
- Dangerous species should affect settlement design and infrastructure (Step 5).
- Symbolic animals should appear in faction culture and NPC beliefs (Step 7).

## Step 4 — Identify Environmental Features

Beyond basic geography: the specific landmarks, hazards, and anomalies that define the world's character and generate story.

### Core Questions

1. What are the **iconic landmarks**? (ruins, mountains, megastructures, sacred sites)
2. What **natural hazards** recur? (earthquakes, toxic rain, void storms, magical surges)
3. Are there **zones of permanent danger**? What makes them impassable or lethal?
4. What **anomalies** exist that don't fit the normal physical rules of the world? (gravity wells, time distortions, pockets of alien physics)
5. What **ruins or remnants** of prior civilisations exist? What do they imply about what came before?
6. How do **environmental hazards** affect daily life, settlement design, and behaviour?
7. Are there **seasonal or cyclical environmental events**? (floods, magical storms, migrations)
8. What **resources or opportunities** are found only in dangerous zones?
9. How do people **navigate or adapt** to the hazards?
10. What **myths or beliefs** have formed around specific environmental features?

### GhostMeshBrain Mapping

Environmental features map directly to GhostMesh agent stressors:
- Radiation zones → heat accumulation
- Toxic areas → waste accumulation
- Supernatural anomalies → surprise/uncertainty terms in active inference
- Hazardous weather → metabolic cost increases

## Step 5 — Establish World Infrastructure and Dynamics

Infrastructure is what organises people. It determines who has power, how resources flow, and what conflicts are structural rather than personal.

### Political Infrastructure

1. What **type of government** or governance structure exists? (empire, democracy, theocracy, warlordism, corporate control, council)
2. How is **political power legitimised**? (heredity, military, divine mandate, meritocracy, wealth)
3. What are the **primary political factions** and their agendas?
4. How is **law enforced**? By whom and with what authority?
5. What are the **major political tensions** that could escalate?

### Economic Infrastructure

6. What is the **primary economic activity**? (agriculture, mining, trade, manufacturing, service, raiding)
7. What **currency or exchange systems** exist? (coin, barter, energy credits, favour economy)
8. Who **controls the means of production** and distribution?
9. What are the **trade routes** and who controls them?
10. What **economic inequalities** are structural and why do they persist?

### Technological / Magical Infrastructure

11. What is the **technological or magical level** of the setting?
12. Who has **access to advanced technology or magic**, and who doesn't?
13. What are the **primary energy sources**?
14. What **infrastructure maintenance** does the population depend on? What happens when it fails?
15. Are there **technological or magical gatekeepers**? What is their social role?

### Social Infrastructure

16. What **social hierarchies** exist and how rigid are they?
17. What are the **mechanisms of social mobility** (or their absence)?
18. How is **knowledge transmitted**? (oral tradition, literacy, institutions, apprenticeship)
19. What **institutions** maintain social cohesion? (religion, military, guilds, family structures)
20. What **marginalised or underclass groups** exist? What systemic factors keep them marginalised?

## Step 6 — Generate Careers from Infrastructures

Careers are not invented — they emerge from the world's needs. This step ensures NPCs have jobs that make sense.

### Method

For each infrastructure system identified in Step 5, ask:

> *"What labour is required to maintain, operate, or exploit this system?"*

Then for each career type:
- What **skills** does it require?
- What **social status** does it carry?
- What is the **daily life** of someone in this role?
- What **career pathways** exist (entry-level → senior → specialist)?
- What are the **failure conditions** — what does unemployment or career disruption look like?

### Career Categories

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Primary (resource extraction) | Farmer, miner, fisher, hunter, logger |
| Secondary (processing/manufacturing) | Smith, miller, brewer, mechanic, fabricator |
| Tertiary (services) | Merchant, guard, healer, priest, teacher, administrator |
| Quaternary (information/expertise) | Researcher, engineer, mage, tactician, hacker |
| Grey/informal | Scavenger, smuggler, mercenary, fence, fixer |
| Extinct/obsolete | Jobs that used to exist and what their loss means |

### NPC Application

For individual NPCs: assign a career that emerges from their faction, region, and social class. The career determines:
- Daily routine and available actions
- Social connections
- Stress vectors (job loss, workplace danger, professional shame)
- Metabolic costs if integrating with GhostMeshBrain

## Step 7 — Determine World Personality

Cultures, factions, and individuals have personality profiles that emerge from their history, environment, and infrastructure. This step codifies that personality so it drives consistent behaviour.

### Cultural Personality Questions

1. What **core values** does the culture prioritise? (honour, efficiency, loyalty, freedom, piety, pragmatism)
2. What are the **taboos** — behaviours that trigger social punishment or ostracism?
3. What is the **attitude toward strangers and outsiders**?
4. How does the culture handle **failure, death, and grief**?
5. What are the **primary forms of celebration and ritual**?
6. What is the **default emotional register** — are people generally anxious, stoic, expressive, guarded?
7. What **myths or founding stories** do people commonly know and reference?
8. How does the culture relate to **its own history**? (pride, shame, denial, obsession)
9. What are the **status markers** — how do people signal class, success, or belonging?
10. What **humour styles** exist? (dark, absurdist, self-deprecating, none tolerated in public)

### Faction Personality (Subset)

Apply the same questions at faction scale, then note:
- How does this faction's personality **diverge from the broader culture**?
- What **in-group vs. out-group** behaviours differ?
- What **propaganda or mythology** does the faction maintain about itself?

### NPC Personality (Subset)

Apply at individual scale, then note:
- What **personality masks** does this NPC wear in different social contexts?
- What **core beliefs** are rigid vs. flexible?
- What **stress triggers** destabilise this NPC?
- What is their **default coping strategy** under pressure?

### GhostMeshBrain Mapping

World/faction personality maps to GhostMesh agent parameters:
- Core values → prior beliefs and action weighting
- Taboos → hard constraints on action selection
- Default emotional register → baseline valence and arousal
- Stress triggers → specific surprise/uncertainty inputs
- Personality masks → context-dependent action policies

## The Expert Review Process

After completing one full loop, stress-test the world with expert-perspective questions. The goal is to find contradictions, implausibilities, and missed consequences.

### Psychology Review

Ask from the perspective of a psychologist:

- Do the described behaviours make sense given the environmental stressors?
- Are the mental health implications of the world's conditions addressed? (trauma, chronic stress, deprivation)
- Do the cultural norms around emotion expression match the survival demands of the environment?
- Are there conditions that would produce mass psychological dysfunction? Are the consequences shown?
- Do individual NPCs have psychologically coherent motivations?

### Sociology Review

Ask from the perspective of a sociologist:

- Are the social hierarchies internally consistent and do they have plausible maintenance mechanisms?
- Are the described social tensions at the right level of conflict — neither too passive nor implausibly stable?
- Are there plausible counter-movements, rebellions, or subcultures challenging the status quo?
- Does the culture's transmission of knowledge match the available infrastructure?
- Are there unintended social consequences of the infrastructure or environment that haven't been addressed?

### Behaviour Analysis Review

Ask from the perspective of a behaviour analyst:

- Do the described career/daily-life routines reinforce the cultural values, or contradict them?
- Are the reinforcement and punishment systems (economic, social, legal) internally consistent?
- What behaviours would the existing contingencies produce that the world doesn't explicitly address?
- Are there structural incentives that would predictably corrupt the institutions described?

### Ecology Review

Ask from the perspective of an ecologist:

- Does the flora/fauna make ecological sense given the climate and terrain?
- Are the food chains described plausible?
- What environmental pressures would the described population density produce over time?
- Are there resource depletion scenarios baked in, or is the world implausibly sustainable?

### History Review

Ask from the perspective of a historian:

- Does the described history have plausible causation chains?
- Are the timelines internally consistent?
- Are there missing historical consequences — things that would have happened given the events described?
- Is the world's relationship to its own history (official narrative vs. suppressed history) internally consistent?

## Loop Iteration Guidelines

### When to Restart the Full Loop

- Major premise change (genre, time period, core conflict)
- Discovery of a fundamental contradiction in infrastructure or ecology
- Significant scaling change (world → area → faction → NPC)

### When to Do a Partial Loop

- Adding a new region: start at Step 2, skip Step 1
- Adding a new faction: start at Step 5, informed by Steps 1–4
- Adding a new NPC: start at Step 6 (career), informed by all prior steps

### Signs a Loop Is Complete Enough

- Every career has an obvious infrastructure origin
- Every cultural value has an environmental or historical explanation
- No expert review question produces an "I haven't thought about that" response
- A random NPC can be generated in under 5 minutes using the world data
- The world produces stories without the creator having to invent them

### Signs Another Iteration Is Needed

- Large regions or populations have no defined careers
- Cultural values contradict the environmental stressors
- Political systems have no plausible maintenance mechanism
- The world feels like a series of unconnected settings rather than one coherent place
