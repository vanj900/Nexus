# Expert Integration — Alignment Checks

This document contains the full expert-perspective question sets used to stress-test a world after each loop. These are not optional polish — they are how you catch structural problems before building on broken foundations.

Apply these checks after completing at least one full Nexus loop. Be honest with your answers. A question you can't answer is a gap in the world.

---

## How to Use This Document

1. Complete a full loop using `NEXUS_FRAMEWORK.md` or `WORLD_OVERVIEW.md`.
2. Open this document and work through each expert section.
3. For each question: answer it, then note whether the answer is **consistent** with your world data or **reveals a gap**.
4. Gaps go into your iteration notes and get addressed in the next loop.
5. Repeat until no new gaps appear.

## Psychology Alignment Check

*Ask these questions as if you are a psychologist reviewing the world for clinical plausibility.*

### Individual Psychology

1. What are the most common **trauma vectors** in this world? (war, famine, violence, displacement, chronic uncertainty)
   - Are the psychological consequences of these traumas visible in how people behave and what institutions exist?

2. What **attachment styles** would the dominant childrearing patterns produce? (secure, anxious, avoidant, disorganised)
   - Are these reflected in how adult NPCs form relationships and respond to loss?

3. What **cognitive distortions** would be adaptive in this world? (paranoia in a surveillance state, magical thinking under uncertainty, black-and-white thinking under tribal conditions)
   - Are these present in how the culture frames problems and enemies?

4. What **mental health conditions** would be endemic given the environmental stressors?
   - Is there infrastructure to address them, or are they untreated/suppressed/stigmatised?

5. What **shame vs. guilt** culture dynamics are at play?
   - Does the culture police external behaviour (shame) or internal state (guilt)? Both?
   - How does this affect NPC motivation and self-justification?

6. What **identity structures** do people rely on? (occupational, familial, ethnic, ideological, religious)
   - What happens to people whose identity structure collapses (job loss, family death, ideological disillusionment)?

7. Are there **psychological safety valves** built into the culture? (rituals, confession, sanctioned violence, intoxicants, art)
   - What happens when these valves are removed or inaccessible?

### Consistency Check

- [ ] Trauma consequences are visible in behaviour, not just described in history.
- [ ] At least one cultural norm exists specifically because of psychological need, not just tradition.
- [ ] NPCs have coherent motivations that trace back to their history and environment.
- [ ] Mental health consequences of environmental stressors are accounted for.

## Sociology Alignment Check

*Ask these questions as if you are a sociologist reviewing the world's social structures.*

### Social Hierarchy

1. What is the **Gini coefficient analogue** for this world? (extreme inequality, moderate, egalitarian)
   - Is this consistent with the described political and economic infrastructure?

2. What are the **specific mechanisms** that maintain the hierarchy? (legal barriers, cultural stigma, economic gatekeeping, violence)
   - Are these mechanisms visible in daily life, or is the hierarchy maintained by magic/handwaving?

3. What are the **pathways of elite reproduction**? How do the powerful ensure their children remain powerful?
   - Are there credible counter-pressures (meritocracy pressures, revolutionary movements, elite defection)?

4. What **counter-cultures or subcultures** exist at the margins?
   - Are they integrated (tolerated safety valve) or truly threatening to the mainstream?

5. What **collective action problems** exist in this world?
   - Are there structural barriers to the underclass organising? What are they?

### Institutions

6. What **institutions** are degraded, corrupt, or captured?
   - What are the visible symptoms of that degradation in daily life?

7. What **informal institutions** exist where formal ones have failed?
   - (Criminal networks filling legal gaps, community healers where medicine is absent, etc.)

8. How is **social trust** calibrated? (high-trust generalised, low-trust particularised, or tribal)
   - Does this match the described history of conflict and cooperation?

9. What **out-groups** are actively scapegoated?
   - Is there a consistent logic to who gets scapegoated and why?

10. How does the society handle **deviance**? (rehabilitation, exile, execution, ignoring)
    - Is this consistent with the described values and power structures?

### Consistency Check

- [ ] Every social hierarchy has an explicit maintenance mechanism.
- [ ] Counter-movements or subcultures are present, even if marginal.
- [ ] At least one institution is visibly corrupt or degraded.
- [ ] Trust levels match the described history of violence and cooperation.

## Behaviour Analysis Alignment Check

*Ask these questions as if you are a behaviour analyst reviewing the world's contingency structures.*

### Reinforcement and Punishment

1. What **behaviours are systematically reinforced** by the economic system?
   - Are these the behaviours the culture claims to value, or do they contradict stated values?

2. What **behaviours are systematically punished**?
   - Are the punishments proportionate, consistent, and administered by credible agents?

3. What **unintended behaviours** does the incentive structure produce?
   - (If hoarding is punished but scarcity is endemic, covert hoarding will be pervasive regardless of law.)

4. What **superstitious behaviours** would the uncertainty structure produce?
   - (When outcomes are unpredictable and high-stakes, people develop rituals. Where are they in this world?)

5. What is the **extinction schedule** for cooperative behaviour?
   - How often does cooperation go unrewarded? Does this match the described trust levels?

### Daily Life and Routine

6. What does a **typical weekday** look like for three different social classes?
   - Are these routines consistent with the careers described in Step 6?

7. What **shaping history** would have produced the current behavioural repertoire of the culture?
   - (Cultures that were historically invaded develop specific vigilance behaviours. What's in this world's history that explains current behaviour patterns?)

8. Are the described **cultural values** actually maintained by contingencies, or are they just stated values that nobody follows?
   - Stated values without contingency support are aspirational at best, propaganda at worst.

9. What **escape and avoidance behaviours** are common?
   - How do ordinary people cope with unavoidable aversives? (intoxicants, religion, violence, dissociation)

10. What **behavioural cusps** exist for NPCs — points where one change unlocks a whole new behavioural repertoire?
    - (Learning to read, gaining access to a weapon, losing a child — what are the cusps for key NPCs?)

### Consistency Check

- [ ] Stated cultural values are backed by actual reinforcement structures.
- [ ] Unintended consequences of the incentive system have been addressed.
- [ ] Escape and avoidance behaviours exist and are tied to specific aversives.
- [ ] NPC daily routines are consistent with their career and social class.

## Ecology Alignment Check

*Ask these questions as if you are an ecologist reviewing the world for biological plausibility.*

### Food Chains and Ecosystems

1. What is the **primary producer base**? (plants, chemosynthetic organisms, magical energy, artificial food synthesis)
   - Is the energy pyramid plausible given the climate and available sunlight/energy?

2. Are the **population sizes** of predator and prey species consistent with each other and with the food base?
   - Apex predators are rare. Are they described as rare?

3. What are the **keystone species** — species whose removal would collapse the ecosystem?
   - Are these identified, and are there narratives around their potential loss?

4. What **ecological pressures** does the described population density create?
   - Deforestation, soil depletion, water table drawdown, pest pressure — where are these in the world?

5. Are there **invasive species dynamics** — species out of their native range disrupting local ecologies?

### Human-Environment Interaction

6. Is the world's **resource use rate** sustainable at the described population and technology level?
   - If not, what does depletion look like and when does it become a crisis?

7. What **environmental diseases** would the described conditions produce?
   - (Dense urban populations without sanitation produce specific diseases. What are this world's endemic diseases?)

8. How does the described **agriculture** interact with the soil and water systems over time?
   - Is there soil erosion? Salinisation? Irrigation dependence?

9. Are **wild zones** maintained, or has human activity eliminated all wilderness?
   - What does the loss of wilderness mean for the described fauna?

10. What **ecological time bombs** exist that the characters might not yet be aware of?

### Consistency Check

- [ ] The food chain is explicitly described and internally consistent.
- [ ] Population size is plausible given the resource base.
- [ ] At least one environmental pressure from human activity is present.
- [ ] Endemic diseases exist and are culturally addressed.

## History Alignment Check

*Ask these questions as if you are a historian reviewing the world's timeline for plausibility.*

### Causation and Consequence

1. Does every major **historical event** have a plausible cause?
   - (Events don't happen in isolation. What caused the war? What caused the collapse? What caused the founding?)

2. Does every major historical event have **plausible consequences** that are visible in the present?
   - (Wars leave refugees, debts, territorial resentments, orphans, and demographic gaps. Where are these?)

3. Are the **timelines internally consistent**?
   - (If the civilisation is 500 years old but the architecture requires 200 years to build, the timeline is broken.)

4. What is the **official history** vs. the **suppressed or forgotten history**?
   - Who benefits from the official narrative? What do they need people to forget?

5. What **historical analogues** does this world draw from?
   - This is a design check: are the analogues acknowledged and deliberately adapted, or is the world accidentally a carbon copy of Earth history with the serial numbers filed off?

### Memory and Myth

6. What **historical myths** have formed around key events?
   - How do these myths differ from what actually happened?

7. What events are in **living memory** for the oldest NPCs?
   - How does living memory create a different relationship to history than official record?

8. What **historical grudges** are still active?
   - Are these grudges plausibly sustained given the described institutions and time elapsed?

9. What **historical technologies or knowledge** have been lost?
   - Loss is normal in history. What was lost and why? Are there people trying to recover it?

10. Is the world **moving somewhere** historically, or is it static?
    - Static worlds feel fake. What are the visible historical trends in motion?

### Consistency Check

- [ ] Every major historical event has an explicit cause.
- [ ] Present conditions reflect historical consequences, not just stated history.
- [ ] Official history and suppressed history are both defined.
- [ ] At least one active historical grudge exists.
- [ ] The world has a direction of travel — it is not frozen in a default state.
